Say you're creating a uniform that's a float between 0 and 1, with a default value of 0.4. It would read like this in the shader code:

    uniform float example_number : hint_range(0.0,1.0) = 0.4;

You could skip giving it a default, and just write this:

Nothing bad happens. You're just now required to set the number in the Inspector if you want the number to be something other than 0, which is what it defaulted to here.

This means the word "hint" isn't really a hint. It's an order. The colon ":" is handing the job off to another party (usually the CPU) to dig up the right uniform. SO WHAT IS A SAMPLER 

A sampler is "more than a texture". If you want a metaphor, consider the telescopes we launch into outer space to take "pictures". I put "pictures" in quotes because they collect raw, machine-readable data; they don't take the beautiful color pictures you'd put up as a poster.

The sensors are able to see all kinds of space-crap, some of which the human eye can see, and other things the human eye can't see. If you want to turn that machine's data into a beautiful color poster for your college dormitory wall to ponder, the people at NASA have to make choices about sampling the image. How do they colorize it? How do they brighten up the contrast? What human-invisible parts do they make visible, and what arbitrary colors do they assign to stuff we normally can't see? The path that data takes from being taken in by the satellite/telescope to becoming your dorm room poster is a similar path taken from the texture through the sampler. Both involve data taking an arbitrary path, colored by an intermediate process, to another form, often a human-legible one. It doesn't have to be meant for human consumption: the sampler might be preparing the data for some other machine process, so "making it visible to people" is just one purpose.

Thus, a depth texture, like all pictures, reflects arbitrary decisions within its presentation. It's a reflection of Godot's choices about how depth should be processed and presented to you. This matters later.

EXPLANATION OF THE DEPTH ASSIGNMENT AND SAMPLER LINE

    float depth = texture(godot_depth_sampler, UV).x;

We are extracting a float and naming it "depth". The texture() function including arguments reads:

texture(godot_depth_sampler, UV)

Note that texture() is *NOT* returning an entire texture. The first argument is the image data from the sampler, and the second argument is the XY coordinate of the sampler's image that you want to pull color data from. 

In plain English, this texture() function is what normal humans would refer to with a function name more like, sample_texture_at_this_UV_coordinate(). But we're not using normal human language in this chapter. 

There's a final swizzle added, the .x :

texture(godot_depth_sampler, UV).x

...because the depth_buffer stores depth in the first channel, sometimes called the r channel (of "rgb") but here it's the x channel, of "xyz". In this particular case, the ".x" does NOT mean a position in XYZ space.

    ALBEDO = vec3(depth);

This final line should be pretty obvious if you've read my other chapters--the float for depth gets pasted into all three color channels as a vec3 and gets spit out as the final color, making a black and white image representing depth.

The depth buffer is expressed by the engine exponentially. This is so that you get more depth accuracy for close objects, at the expense of depth accuracy for far objects.

This is Claude's suggested code for converting that exponential version into a linear version:

    uniform float far_distance : hint_range(0.0,100.0) = 30;

    float linear_depth(vec2 uv, mat4 inv_proj) {
        float d = texture(godot_depth_sampler, uv).x;
        vec3 ndc = vec3(uv * 2.0 - 1.0, d);
        vec4 view = inv_proj * vec4(ndc, 1.0);
        view.xyz /= view.w;
        return -view.z;

MATRIX MULTIPLICATION: IT'S NOT MULTIPLICATION 

And this is where I give my middle finger to linear algebra textbooks everywhere, which is that the thing everybody calls "matrix multiplication" isn't multiplication. It's just a useful function we run on two matrices.

This helps explain why matrix multiplication doesn't follow the rules of multiplication. Normally in algebra:

    2 * 3 = 3 * 2 = 6.

It's symmetric. The math word is "commutative". Matrix "multiplication" is not. A times B does not equal B times A when dealing with matrix "multiplication".

Also, as we saw above, you really actually can multiply two vectors together. Can you multiply two vectors together with matrix "multiplication"?

No. You can't. Matrix multiplication requires the number of *columns* in the *first* matrix to match the number of *rows* in the *second* matrix. That's why you can't do this:

[1,2] * [3,4]

But you can do this:

[1,2] * [3]
        [4]

The math of that operation is this:

[1*3 + 2*4] = 3 + 8 = 11

The number 11 looks familiar, doesn't it?

SO WHAT IS IT, REALLY?

If they're being honest, some math teachers will instead refer to it as a "function" (it is!) or that it developed to express a "chain of functions" (also accurate!). The main reason matrix "multiplication" is useful is because functions are useful. Functions can be chained together. Functions can be consolidated. And in graphics programming, consolidating functions into more efficient versions means: "more FPS more more more now!"

Let's say we have a scale(), rotate(), and translate() function that each move vertices around. Matrix multiplication allows you to combine all three functions into one function, like a scale_rotate_translate() function, in a way that can be computed very quickly.

That way, you don't have to do the scale function first and then the rotate function second and the translate function third, repeating math all along the way. You can just do it all at once. When running that operation on millions of polygons at 60+ frames per second for every pixel on the monitor, that... adds up!

FUN FACT ABOUT MATRIX MULTIPLICATION: IT'S WRITTEN BACKWARDS

There are many rakes hiding in the ground when it comes to matrices. Among the most tedious is that the standard convention for writing matrix multiplication functions is to write them *backwards*, and as I mentioned above, you get a different result if the matrix multiplication happens in the wrong order. Also: Not everyone writing on the internet follows that convention, so some people write them forwards. Which means you don't actually know for sure what order it's supposed to be in, you have to figure it out from context.

The logic for this comes from the fact that matrix multiplication was always a function in disguise. So you might see someone in an internet forum say: 

    "Vectors are first multiplied by scale, then by rotation, then by the translation matrix, and thus the equation is T*R*S*V".

That person is definitely using the backwards notation. But the only way you can be absolutely dead sure of that is that they literally just told you the order they were working in.

And if you're accustomed to reading left to right, you might go, wait what? Shouldn't it be V*S*R*T? Am I going crazy?

You're not crazy! Instead, imagine it like the programming functions you're used to:

    result=T(R(S(V)))

From programming or algebra, you'd know that the things inside the parentheses happen first. The scale function would happen first, followed by the R function, and finishing with the T function.

Matrix multiplication works the same way but nobody writes parentheses to clarify anything, causing misery and confusion the world over.

SOME NOTES ABOUT THE INNER OUTER RULES 

Matrices have shapes, and that shape is their number of rows and columns; the first number is the row, the second number is the column. A 2x4 matrix has two rows and four columns.

The inner numbers must match for a valid multiplication, so you can do this:

    [2 rows, 324 columns] x [324 rows, 8 columns]

...but not this, because 6 doesn't equal 4:

    [2 rows, 6 columns] x [4 rows, 2 columns]

The shape of the final matrix is determined by the outside numbers. So this:

  [2 rows, 324 columns] x [324 rows, 8 columns]

...produces a result matrix with 2 rows, and 8 columns.

The matrices that do scaling/rotation/translation are 4x4 matrices; that shape is mostly true everywhere now.

What's NOT true everywhere is whether a vector is expressed as a row or a column.

Say we have a three dimensional vector at (2,3,4,1). That fourth dimension, w, is always 1 and is an arbitrary element I'll discuss later. Should the matrix to express the vector be a single column:

As mentioned above, the matrix multiplication is really this pattern:

You might know from the parentheses of algebra or the basics of programming that func4(argument) gets evaluated first. Then func3(), then func2(), and finally, func1().

This is another reason not to think of matrix "multiplication" as multiplication. It's a function. If these were functions, and you need them to evaluate in the order of scale, then rotation, then translation, you'd write it like this:

    T(R(S(argument)))

And you'd evaluate it just like a programmer would.

So you'd evaluate R(S()) first, then finish with T().

Although I suppose you could always look it up, the T(R(S(V))) pattern, when consolidating, follows the same order that S(V) did. The argument of the function came second and the matrix identified by the function came first.

WHAT IS DIVISION/INVERSION?

As mentioned previously, we're not actually multiplying, when doing matrix "multiplication", so it should come as no surprise that you can't actually divide. "Division" in this case is just another way of saying undoing the effects of the function. This is also called inverting. So if you created the inverse of that SRT matrix, doing the multiplication/dot-producting of that inverse would restore you to your original position.

Consider our translation matrix T:
[1, 0, 0, 4]
[0, 1, 0, 5]
[0, 0, 1, 0]
[0, 0, 0, 1]

We already know that the 4,5,0 numbers in the top right are what do the translating. Which means the inverse of the translation matrix is to undo that transform. If the previous matrix translates things 4 units along X and 5 units along Y, then you move it back negative 4 units on X and negative 5 units on Y, by negating the 4 and the 5:

[1, 0, 0, -4]
[0, 1, 0, -5]
[0, 0, 1,  0]
[0, 0, 0,  1]

You may have figured out the scale matrix just from looking at it. The rotation one is the only hard one, as it requires geometry (or you just memorize or look up the formula if you need it). So creating the inversions of those isn't too crazy either. Conversely, creating the inverse of a matrix that doesn't fit into these particular patterns is difficult and requires a lot of unrelated math.

The notation of inversions is a little funny. The inverse of T is written as T^-1, just as 1/x = x^-1. The notation thus keeps the written pattern of algebraic division, but its only a philosophical similarity: Just as we were never really multiplying when using the multiplication symbol, we're not really dividing when using the division symbol. We're just undoing the function.

SEPARATING THE SCALE/ROTATION/TRANSFORM MATRIX FROM THE MODEL/VIEW/PROJECTION MATRIX 

There's a whole lot of matrices running around! Which ones are which?

When you first create a mesh, in Blender or whatever, it has an origin of 0,0,0. So long as it's the only object in a scene, its origin will always be 0,0,0, forever. Its scale, rotation, and position are all meaningless--the only thing with relevance is the individual polygons' relationship to each other. If you were just floating through an empty void, with nothing else in the universe, how big would you be? What direction would you be pointed in? Your hands would be in a different place than your feet, but inside the void, that's it. *You* wouldn't have a direction, *you* wouldn't have a size, and *you* wouldn't have a position. These only become things when you join a universe of other objects. When you are in the void, you are in local space, sometimes called object space.

But let's say you join a universe, a whole world with other objects. You're no longer at the center/origin of said universe. Maybe you're 100 meters away from the center, and looking away, and quite small compared to everything else. We need a set of functions to move all of your vertices together, in one swift motion, to scale and turn and position them. That's through a combination of the scale/rotation/translation matrix multiplication functions. You are the object. The name of the combined matrix that does all of this at once: the object matrix. It positions you and your vertices in this new space you've found yourself in, called "world space".

God walks through this universe, among its objects. Aside from being a female, She's also a cat, with only one eye. She does not sit at the universe's origin--She prefers to move around, being a cat and all. But since She's the metaphorical center of the universe, and all that matters is what She can see, we have to relocate literally everything else in the universe--rotation and translation included--to make Her, and more specifically, Her one remaining eyeball, the center of the universe. Whatever Her eyeball's rotation and translation, relative to the universe's origin, must be undone to make Her the center. To see the world through Her eye, we must apply a different matrix. The inversion of the rotation and translation matrices of the camera, when combined into one matrix, is: the view matrix. This is view space.

But: God does not see things without perspective. God is an artist. There are vanishing lines in her vision. Things further away are smaller than things that are close, but, how much smaller? Does she see things in widescreen, and if so, how wide? How far does she see into the distance? If she chooses to ignore things too close to her eyeball, like specks of dust an inch or two away, what is the cutoff? Does she pack a great deal into her vision, to see things on both sides, or is her field of view more narrow? (Is God a competitive, first-person shooter?) Perhaps we can't see things as God can, but if we could, then everything needs to be multiplied by another matrix: the projection matrix. Everything that God cannot see is irrelevant--clipped from existence, at least for this frame. Perhaps the physics engine is still keeping track of it (some other, lesser god, in this metaphor) but not in view of the one true God. This is clip space.

Your monitor is composed of thousands of little pixels, each staring into their little portion of God's clip space. They see a portion of a mesh; a triangle! A vertex hither, thither, and fro. Each pixel has a color, a normal, and well of interior thoughts. The pixel assesses the values of each of the three vertices, and chooses an interpolated blend, based on which one is closest, which furthest, and by how far. This is screen space.
